Introduction

Curriculum change can be a blessing for your students. It provides you with an opportunity to keep your program current, to trim fat, and to organize your courses in a way that will be meaningful and logical.

However…

You will find that students benefit from having a stable curriculum and that too many changes can hamper their ability to navigate your program.

Curriculum change is probably the most complex knot you can get yourself tied up in, and it creates short- and long-term impacts for yourself and for you students.

In this notebook, we have attempted to summarize information about curriculum change. While we have attempted to cover many topics, we know we have not covered every contingency and issue. Your Associate Dean of Curriculum, the Dean, individuals in SRR, and current and former chairs should be considered essential resources as you move forward with curriculum change.

Involve SRR (Laurie Williams, williamsla@byui.edu) - If you have questions or if what you are doing seems complicated -- this is best done earlier rather than later.

If you have questions that involve degree audits or iPlan, contact Parker Johnson in SRR.

Planning Change

Courses and Programs must have an Outcomes centric design. The syllabus or program justification must show how the outcomes are central and are incorporated into the course or program design.

Key assessments must be tied to each outcome and be common between sections. Each outcome should have a way of indicating how well it is being met. These assessments need to be sustainable and specific enough for improvements to be made in a periodic cycle. An assessment calendar must be included as part of the plan for programs.

No net new course or programs. New program may be approved if they are fully nested in existing program.

Programs should have a simple design. Emphasis areas and major electives should be minimized or eliminated so students can focus on key core concepts. Cross-disciplinary connections should be made to reduce duplication of classes and increase appropriate breadth in degrees.

Programs are to take a holistic approach by tying all pieces of the degree together. It is preferred that substantive changes "come forward during program reviews instead of each year." The purpose for this is to ensure changes have been thoughtfully considered, advisory boards consulted, and to improve stability in curriculum for students. Nest or stack curriculum elements in ways that show deliberate design to bless the lives of all students.

All courses included in a program proposal outside the sponsoring department need to show that the other department has been contacted and agrees with the class being included in or removed from the program.

Development of programs and even courses should be done in consultation with those in industry who are most likely to hire our students and who understand what the students need. All programs should have an Advisory Council (good use of alumni) made-up of the experts who see where the job market is going keep program credits within the limit for the currently designated degree. Guiding Principles

The following are principles to help guide curriculum decisions. The first set of principles came from a visit of the Executive Committee of the Board to BYU-Idaho in September 2017 and came from discussions had around curriculum. These have not been defined but should give us all the opportunity to ponder what we are doing and how we are going about it.

  1. Responsive Curricula
  2. Set rules for simplification
  3. Stackable curricula
  4. Sequenced modularity
  5. Elegant simplicity balanced with flexibility and comprehensibility for students
  6. One of the constant questions we must ask ourselves: Should we be doing this (course, program, major)?
  7. Importance of pruning – letting in the light vs extensive coverage of content
  8. Importance of teaching practical skills
  9. Importance of teaching eternal knowledge

Standards

The following standards will be the criteria by which all curricular changes will be evaluated.

  1. Alignment with Guiding Principles – Justification information should reflect this alignment.
  2. Student-Learning Outcome centric design of courses and programs – The syllabus or program justification must show how the student-learning outcomes are central and are incorporated into the course or program design. These student-learning outcomes should include both content and life-skills and should be mapped to the program student-learning outcomes.
  3. Key assessments tied to each student-learning outcome – Each student-learning outcome should have a way of indicating how well it is being met. These assessments need to be sustainable and specific enough for improvements to be made in a periodic cycle. A well vetted map (course or program) must be included to show what assessments are being used to show outcome attainment at the course level and how all the courses fit together to achieve outcomes at the program level.
  4. BYU-Idaho Learning Outcomes (ILOs) – These are outcomes we expect of all students who graduate from BYU-Idaho. They are measured at two levels – Within General Education and within each academic program. All the ILOs will be assessed in addition to the PLOs. Common rubrics for assessing the ILOs. The hope is that many, if not all courses will help students develop these skills and make that development visible to the students.

    5. Program specific standards

  5. Modular program design – Nesting or stacking of curriculum elements in ways that show deliberate design to bless the lives of all students.
  6. Collaboration - All courses included in a program proposal outside the sponsoring department need to show that the other department has been contacted and agrees with the class being included in or removed from the program.
  7. Certificate - Proposals must fit the following criteria:
    1. Builds toward an employer identified skill set that will give students an advantage in the job market.
    2. Has well defined outcomes and assessments that have been vetted and mapped and measure job skill development that is the focus of the certificate.
    3. Must be between 12-15 credits. No option can take a student below 12 credits or above 15 credits.
    4. Should be a specific set of classes with no or very limited optional classes.
    5. If it is designated as an advanced certificate, the prerequisites must be included in the program of study.
    6. Must be fully nested in an associate degree or bachelor’s degree. This means that the certificate cannot have options that are not in the degree and the degree cannot have options that are not in the certificate.

    8. Additional certificate considerations:

    • Certificates will be evaluated during regular program reviews to determine if they are still meeting the established criteria and outcomes are being measured and met.
    • Use other terms (module, cluster) to distinguish parts of a degree that do not fit the definition of a certificate.
    • Certificates will not be “officially” awarded unless at least 6 credits are completed with BYU-Idaho coursework.
    • Students can add a certificate with elective credits, like they would add a minor, if the certificate is outside their major.
    • Students cannot “declare” a certificate as their only path of study and be eligible for federal financial aid.
    • Subject to approval by Curriculum Council, Deans Council, President’s Council, the board, and NWCCU.
  8. Cluster – 12-15 credits, mostly used as an advisory tool, not recognized by the NWCCU, approvals with UCC, No diploma, No PLOs, No Program Review.
  9. Concentration – 27-33 credits, Group of courses with a concentrated focus within a discipline, linked to the IDS degree program, not recognized by the NWCCU, Approvals with UCC and DC, No diploma, No PLOs, No Program Review.
  10. Cluster – 12-15 credits, mostly used as an advisory tool, not recognized by the NWCCU, approvals with UCC, No diploma, No PLOs, No Program Review.
  11. Concentration – 27-33 credits, Group of courses with a concentrated focus within a discipline, linked to the IDS degree program, not recognized by the NWCCU, Approvals with UCC and DC, No diploma, No PLOs, No Program Review.
  12. Minor – 18-25 credits, group of courses to give students a broad understanding across a discipline, recognized but not reported to NWCCU yet, Approvals with UCC, PEG, CES, and the Board, No diploma, does show on the transcript, PLOs not required yet, No Program Review yet.
  13. Emphasis area – Intended to provide minor additional focus to an existing degree. Should be a small subset of the program (preferably 9-15 credits). We have been asked to limit these.
  14. Standard bachelor’s degree – 40-55 credits.
  15. Integrated standard degree – 56-67 credits, which is a standard degree plus 12-15 credits from another area. Limited to those who show a strong need for a particular combination of areas.
  16. Specialized degree – Limited to those with external accreditation requirements that are mandatory.
  17. Education degrees – These have very strict rules but do not fit any of the above categories exactly.

  19. Eliminate duplication of courses across departments – Departments work together to best meet the needs of students with the goal of minimizing or eliminating duplication.

Required course travel – If travel for a course requires students to spend time outside of regularly scheduled class times, it must be in the syllabus, be required of all sections, and demonstrate how it is necessary to meet course outcomes. For the benefit of students and colleagues across campus, this should be kept to a minimum, focusing only on the most necessary experiences.
  22. Course fees – Should be minimized and must meet fee guidelines.
  23. For courses which have multiple sections, common Student-Learning Outcomes, and Key assessments, which indicate common outcome attainment, must be the same. Teaching pedagogy can vary between sections and modes of delivery. Our curriculum management tool (CMT) will be the official repository of all common Student-Learning Outcomes. Outcomes Rubrics

Process

"Departments will be responsible to make sure all proposals have complete documentation and rationale and show appropriate fit for the department. They can do this with a curriculum committee if they choose, but the department process will be left to the discretion of the Department Chair (Department Chairs give approval in our CMT)."1

Step 1 - Course Changes

  • In winter (year 1) you will submit changes to courses.
  • Changes to a course might include things like: 1. a change in the description, 2. credit hours, 3. prerequisites, or 4. course learning outcomes.
  • A change to credit hours and contact hours impact each program within which the course resides and it impacts FLUs. You will need to do an evaluation of FLUs if you change credit or contact hours.
  • Rule of thumb 1: you will not be allowed to change the content of a course, or its outcomes, more than about 30%. A change of more than 30% requires that the course be deleted and a new course created.
  • Rule of thumb 2: you will not be allowed to add a course unless you delete a course (net-zero).

Step 2 - Program Changes

  • In Spring (year 1) you will submit changes to programs.
  • Changes to a program might include things like a change to the course credit hours because a course in the program was canceled or added.

Step 3 - Changes are reviewed by University

During Spring (yr 1), Fall (yr 1), and Winter (yr 2), the changes move from the Curriculum Council to a University Academic Council and to various departments for review. After approval, changes are input into the catalog system.

"Once approved at the department level, curriculum will be vetted at the college level. Each college will create a college curriculum council that will be responsible to review all proposals within its college. This council will look at need, fit, relevance, resource requests, and adherence to all curriculum guidelines. Most curriculum proposals will be approved at this level with guidance from the Curriculum Executive Council to ensure consistency across the University and compatibility with current systems. When a proposal requires additional review, the Associate Dean requests a full University Curriculum Council review (Associate Dean for Curriculum gives approval in our CMT)."1

"New programs, IDS changes, General Education changes, fee changes, and increased resource requests may require additional approval at the Deans Council, President’s Council, Board of Trustees, and NWCCU level before any implementation can take place."1

There are multiple software products that work together and some that do not work well with one another.

  1. The catalog is built from Kuali.
  2. Information in the catalog (via Kuali) must be hand-entered into CX (Jenzebar) and then from there filters into the grad planner, degree audit, registration, and graduation. Human errors happen so work patiently with those who are entering changes. It is quite a process because there is no API between CX and Kuali.

Step 4 - Changes are approved and published

Changes are published in the Spring Catalog (yr 2) that is used by students to sign up for Spring courses (yr 2).

Timing - The Curriculum Consequence Cat and His Long Tail

If your changes are wrong or ill advised, you and your students will live with it for a long time.

Changing the curriculum is quite a long and involved process. Most important for you to remember is that a change made today does not impact students until about one year after you made the change, but once the change has been implemented it has ramifications for many semesters. The following image attempts to visually display how long a change remains on the system. Changing a course or a program starts in the first year (first row) and is not approved until the sixth semester after the change was started. If the change does not accomplish what you intended, students will live with the change for at least five to six semesters before the change can be reversed. Once reversed, the change will reside in the older catalogs for seven years.
